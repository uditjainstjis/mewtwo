Deep Understanding of the Synapta Framework
The Synapta project constitutes a pre-registered empirical investigation into prompt-level
multi-adapter Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) composition, optimized for memory-constrained

generalization during inference without incurring the computational overhead of full
fine-tuning or the memory redundancy of deploying multiple monolithic large language models
(LLMs). Operating under severe constraints—an Apple M3 processor with 8GB of unified
memory—the architecture limits itself to 4-bit quantized base models (Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct
and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3) and prompt-level routing heuristics.1

Explicit and Hidden Assumptions
The architecture is predicated on several critical assumptions that dictate its operational
bounds and theoretical validity.
The primary explicit assumption is the hypothesis of linear subspace composability. The
framework posits that the linear combination of distinct, independently trained LoRA adapter
outputs directly correlates with a coherent semantic blending of domain knowledge. It
explicitly assumes that the knowledge embedded within the Medical Diagnosis adapter and the
Mathematics adapter resides in non-interfering, additive parameter subspaces, allowing their
simultaneous activation to resolve intersectional queries. A secondary explicit assumption is
the necessity of magnitude constraint. The implementation of the Weight-Cap and Norm-Ratio
clamping mechanisms explicitly assumes that the primary failure mode of multi-adapter
composition is activation magnitude explosion—specifically, that unconstrained addition injects
excessive energy into the residual stream, causing the model to diverge from its pre-trained
manifold.1
Beneath the explicit architectural choices lie profound hidden assumptions. The most
structurally limiting is the assumption of prompt-level homogeneity. By assigning a static
routing weight
for the entirety of a generative sequence, the system inherently assumes
that the necessity for specific domain knowledge is uniformly distributed across all tokens. It
assumes a token predicting a mathematical operator requires the exact same degree of legal
expert activation as a token predicting a statutory definition. Furthermore, the evaluation
framework harbors a hidden assumption regarding metric sufficiency. By relying strictly on
cosine similarity derived from a 22-million parameter sentence transformer (all-MiniLM-L6-v2)
to evaluate performance on multi-domain expert queries 1, the framework assumes that lexical
and distributional semantic overlap is a valid, high-fidelity proxy for logical reasoning accuracy,
factual correctness, and multi-step deduction.

Metric Inadequacy and Unpublishability
The framework's fatal flaw regarding publishability is its evaluation protocol. The study relies on
semantic similarity using a 22-million parameter sentence transformer (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) to
evaluate the outputs of a 1.5B and 7B parameter model.1 This metric is fundamentally
inadequate for evaluating advanced capabilities. Semantic similarity measures lexical
distribution and bag-of-words overlap; it is entirely blind to logical soundness, factual accuracy,
and mathematical correctness.
In the current LLM evaluation frontier (2025–2026), evaluating multi-domain logic requires

The Evolution of Parameter-Space Merging
The earliest attempts to solve cross-task adapter interference (2023–2024) operated directly in
the weight space. Methods such as Task Arithmetic, TIES-Merging, and DARE hypothesized that
interference was caused by parameter redundancy and sign conflicts.10 TIES-Merging
implemented a "TRIM, ELECT SIGN & MERGE" protocol, dropping low-magnitude parameters
and resolving conflicting update directions via majority vote.12 DARE extended this by applying
random dropout to delta weights followed by magnitude rescaling.10 AdaMerging advanced
the paradigm by learning scaling coefficients directly from unlabeled test sets using
unsupervised entropy minimization, adapting the merge dynamically.13
However, these weight-space methods suffer from a fundamental limitation: they operate in
the full parameter space (

adapters.15 When TIES-Merging sparsifies a reconstructed matrix

Directional Anisotropy and Token-Level Dynamics (2026)

While subspace alignment solved the basis mismatch, it did not solve the routing granularity or
the directional sensitivity imbalance.
1.​ Addressing Anisotropy (TARA): The 2026 paper "Preference-Aligned LoRA Merging"
(CVPR) identified that even with sufficient subspace coverage, task losses exhibit unequal
sensitivity to different LoRA directions (anisotropy). TARA mitigates this by aligning
merging weights using a preference-weighted cross-entropy pseudo-loss, ensuring
direction-wise reweighting that preserves task-relevant subspaces.4
2.​ Token-Level Efficiency (CoMoL): The limitation of prompt-level routing has been
entirely bypassed by Core Space Mixture of LoRA (CoMoL, arxiv 2026). Traditional
MoE-LoRA architectures compute full
transformations for each expert, resulting
in severe memory bloat and latency. CoMoL solves this by performing the token-level
routing exclusively within the compact core space.24 Instead of aggregating
high-dimensional expert outputs, CoMoL dynamically weights the small
core
matrices based on the current token's hidden state, merging them into a single core
matrix before the main transformation.25 This maintains the FLOP profile of a standard
LoRA forward pass while achieving the expressivity of token-level MoE.

Real and Unsolved Gaps
Despite the synthesis of core-space merging and token-level routing, a critical gap remains at
the frontier: Real-time, long-horizon reasoning synthesis over orthogonal manifolds.
Current token-level models (like CoMoL) route based purely on the immediate hidden state of
a single token. They operate via System 1 pattern matching. However, complex multi-domain
queries require System 2 deliberation—planning, searching, verifying, and backtracking across
different domain subspaces. Furthermore, hardware utilization on single-GPU high-VRAM
setups (like the RTX 5090) for dense token-level core-space MoE remains highly
memory-bound, requiring specialized Triton or FlashAttention-3 kernels to maximize FP8 Tensor
Core throughput without saturating the VRAM bandwidth.27 The challenge is bridging the
mathematical elegance of orthonormal core spaces with the agentic, test-time compute
scaling required for advanced logical deduction.

Iteration Strategy
Scientific breakthroughs are rarely achieved on the first training run; they require pivoting
intelligently based on empirical telemetry.
●​ Scenario A: Success in Accuracy, Failure in Latency.
○​ Observation: The mathematical framework of Core Space Merging is validated
(reasoning metrics improve), but the token-level dynamic calculation exceeds the
20% latency overhead bound.
○​ Pivot: We must distill the routing probabilities. We will utilize the trained token-level
router to label a massive dataset, and subsequently train a static, prompt-level
predictor. This predictor will output a sequence of discrete expert states ahead of
time (generating a "Routing Plan"). We can then inject the experts sequentially at the
prompt generation phase using standard, highly optimized FlashAttention-3 kernels,
separating the routing logic from the critical generation path.27
●​ Scenario B: Perfect Domain Isolation, Failure in Reasoning Synthesis.
○​ Observation: The model successfully isolates domains (zero interference on
single-domain tasks) and recalls isolated facts, but fails to logically link them on
GPQA or SWE-Bench. The fundamental assumption that adding independent
domain parameter knowledge equates to reasoning is proven false.
○​ Pivot: Shift the paradigm entirely from Parameter Merging to Activation Steering
(Representation Engineering). Apply "Conditional Activation Steering" techniques.29
Instead of training matrices to recall domain facts, we will train task vectors that
steer the model's intermediate hidden states toward specific logical operations (e.g.,
"deduction step," "verification step," "abstraction step").
●​ Scenario C: Phase 1 Orthonormal Bases Fail to Capture Features.
○​ Observation: The shared Stiefel manifold training fails to converge on a globally
useful basis for 20 disparate domains.
○​ Pivot: Implement TARA (Preference-Aligned LoRA Merging).4 We abandon the
pre-training geometric constraint. We train standard, independent LoRAs, but
merge them post-hoc using the TARA preference-weighted cross-entropy
pseudo-loss. This algorithm projects out the anisotropic "intruder dimensions"
before inference, forcing alignment retrospectively rather than prescriptively.

By abandoning naive algebraic addition and fully embracing manifold-constrained core-space
optimization and token-level dynamics, this experimental blueprint transitions the work from a
heavily constrained edge-device engineering exercise to a profound theoretical advancement
at the absolute frontier of machine learning reasoning architectures.
